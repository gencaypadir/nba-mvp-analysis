01 · Introduction

The Question

Shai Gilgeous-Alexander won the 2025 Kia NBA MVP award, but was he truly the best player of the season? Many analysts and fans argued Nikola Jokić deserved the honor, with others pointing to breakout performances across the league.

This project strips away narratives, media hype, and voting bias to find the statistically best player of the 2024–25 season. We built a composite scoring system using per-game averages, percentile rankings, strength-of-schedule adjustments, and minutes weighting to produce an objective final ranking.

Our tasks:

  • Rank all 569 NBA players by composite statistical performance
  • Adjust scores for strength of schedule and playing time
  • Visualize the top candidates across multiple stat dimensions
  • Track performance consistency across the season
  • Reveal who the data says is the "Real MVP"
02 · Dataset

The Data

We used a publicly available NBA player game log dataset from the 2024–25 regular season, sourced from Kaggle and licensed under MIT. The dataset covers October 22, 2024 through February 7, 2025, representing approximately 60% of the regular season. Each row represents a single player's performance in a single game. While this partial-season snapshot does not capture late-season surges or slumps, it provides a robust sample of over 16,000 game logs for meaningful statistical analysis.

03 · Methodology

How We Scored

1

Per-Game Averages

We aggregated every game log into per-game averages across 20 statistical categories for each player, giving us a single row per player that represents their typical performance.

2

Percentile Ranking

Each player's averaged stats were converted into percentile ranks (0 to 100) across 15 key categories: shooting volume (FG, 3P, FT), shooting efficiency (FG%, 3P%, FT%), rebounds (ORB, DRB, TRB), assists, steals, blocks, turnovers, points, and game score. Turnovers were inverted so that fewer turnovers produce a higher percentile. These 15 percentiles were then averaged into a single Composite Score.

3

Games Played Filter

We filtered to players with at least 39 games played, which represents 60% of the dataset's coverage. This removes small-sample outliers like players who appeared in only a handful of games and might have inflated or deflated stats.

4

Strength of Schedule (SOS)

We calculated each team's win percentage from actual game results in the dataset, then used those win percentages to measure schedule difficulty. For each team, we averaged the win percentages of every opponent they faced. A higher number means that team played against stronger opponents on average, while a lower number means an easier schedule.

5

SOS Adjustment (15% Weight)

We divided each team's SOS by the league average SOS to get a ratio (the "SOS factor"). A team with a tougher-than-average schedule gets a factor above 1.0, while an easier schedule produces a factor below 1.0. We then blended this factor with a neutral value of 1.0 using a 15% weight: multiplier = 0.15 × SOS_factor + 0.85. This means 85% of the score stays untouched and only 15% is influenced by schedule difficulty. For example, a player on Toronto (hardest schedule, SOS factor ~1.09) gets roughly a 1.4% boost, while a player on New York (easiest schedule, SOS factor ~0.92) receives about a 1.2% penalty. The adjustment is deliberately small so it nudges scores without overpowering the raw stats.

6

Minutes Adjustment (15% Weight)

We calculated each player's average minutes per game and divided it by their team's average minutes per player to get a "minutes factor." Starters who play 36 minutes on a team averaging 24 per player get a factor around 1.5, while a bench player at 12 minutes gets a factor around 0.5. Like the SOS adjustment, we blended this with a 15% weight: multiplier = 0.15 × MP_factor + 0.85. This gives starters a noticeable boost (a factor of 1.5 translates to roughly a 7.5% increase) and applies a similar penalty to low-minute players. The logic is simple: players who are on the court more carry greater responsibility and their stats reflect a larger role on their team.

10 · Findings

What the Data Says

01

Jokić is the statistical MVP, by a clear margin

Across every composite measure we built (raw percentile, SOS-adjusted, and minutes-weighted) Nikola Jokić ranked first. His uniquely balanced statistical profile, elite efficiency, and performance against quality competition produced a Final Score 3.5 points ahead of the actual MVP winner. The data strongly supports the argument that the award should have gone to Denver.

02

Volume scoring alone does not predict MVP quality

Several high-scoring guards ranked outside the top 10 once efficiency, rebounding, and schedule difficulty were factored in. The scatter plot confirms that points per game without efficiency is a misleading signal. True MVP candidates must balance volume with FG% and contribute across multiple statistical dimensions rather than excelling in just one.

03

Context adjustments reveal hidden value

The SOS and minutes adjustments, while deliberately subtle (15% weight each), meaningfully reshaped the rankings. Players like Cade Cunningham and Kyrie Irving saw some of the largest boosts (+6.5 and +6.6 points respectively) because they logged heavy starter minutes on their teams while facing above-average schedules. Meanwhile, players on teams with easier opponents saw smaller gains. These adjustments ensure that a dominant stat line against weak competition is valued differently than one earned against playoff-caliber teams, and that a starter carrying 36 minutes of responsibility is distinguished from a role player putting up numbers in limited action.

Limitations & Future Work

This analysis covers approximately 60% of the 2024–25 regular season (October 22, 2024 through February 7, 2025), which may not capture late-season performance surges or slumps. Future iterations could incorporate defensive metrics (DPOY voting data, defensive rating), playoff performance, and on/off court impact data to build a more comprehensive model. A machine learning approach, similar to the Samford University and Wharton studies referenced, could also weight statistical categories based on historical MVP voting patterns rather than equal weighting.
